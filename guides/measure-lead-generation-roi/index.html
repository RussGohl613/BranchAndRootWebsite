How to Measure Lead Generation ROI From a New Hire

Branch and Root Consulting · Orange County, CA · Updated June 2026

Lead generation ROI is calculated as (revenue attributable to the hire − fully-loaded cost of the hire) ÷ fully-loaded cost of the hire, expressed as a percentage. A hire who generates $120,000 in attributable revenue against $60,000 in fully-loaded annual cost produces a 100% ROI — meaning you doubled the investment. The formula is simple; the hard part is defining “fully-loaded cost” and “attributable revenue” honestly — and most owners can’t do either calculation because nobody built the measurement layer that ties a closed job back to the hire who originated it.

What goes into fully-loaded cost

Most owners undercount the cost side. A fully-loaded calculation includes base salary, employer-side payroll taxes and benefits (which add 20–30% to base in most US markets — a $50,000 salary commonly costs $60,000–$65,000 when fully burdened), tool licenses (CRM seats, dialers, list tools), recruiting and onboarding costs amortized over 12 months, and your own management time. For a full-time outbound hire at a local service business, fully-loaded annual cost commonly lands in the $65,000–$85,000 range. Use that number as your denominator, not base salary alone — operators who have done this before keep a running cost worksheet updated monthly so the number is never reconstructed from memory at year-end.

How to define attributable revenue

Revenue attribution is the other side of the equation, and it requires agreeing on a clear rule before the hire starts — not after a dispute about credit. Operators who do this well define attribution in writing on day one and paste it into the hire’s onboarding doc; everyone else has the argument at month three when the first commission check is in question.

The most defensible method for a local service business is lead-source tagging: every job in your CRM carries a lead source field, and the resulting revenue is summed by source at month end. Three rules to define in advance: how long after first contact a closed job still counts as attributable (one week for an emergency repair, potentially months for a large installation); whether the hire gets full credit when you close the appointment they set (most owners say yes, since appointment creation is the function being measured); and whether repeat business from an originated customer counts (typically no — keep first-job attribution clean).

A worked example with round numbers

The numbers below are illustrative only — they are constructed to show the math clearly, not to represent actual benchmarks for your industry or market.

Suppose you hire an outbound specialist at $50,000 base salary. With employer-side costs and tool licenses, the fully-loaded annual cost is $68,000. During the first year, the hire sets 180 qualified appointments. Your close rate on those appointments is 40%, producing 72 closed jobs. Your average job revenue is $2,000, so attributable annual revenue is $144,000.

Plugging into the formula: ($144,000 − $68,000) ÷ $68,000 = 112% ROI — the hire more than doubled its cost in year one. Now drop the close rate from 40% to 20% because follow-up is inconsistent after the appointment is set: attributable revenue falls to $72,000, ROI drops to 6%, barely breakeven. Lead gen ROI is highly sensitive to what happens after the appointment is booked, which is why the post on diagnosing a lead gen vs. sales problem is worth reading before drawing conclusions from the numbers.

From the Field · Branch & Root

When we set up reporting for a lead gen program, the ROI calculation runs automatically each month — every closed job tagged to its origin, every cost line tracked, the denominator always current. The owner sees the number without pulling records or reconstructing anything by hand. That visibility is what makes the calculation useful rather than theoretical.

Payback period: a simpler lens for the first year

ROI percentages are useful for comparison, but payback period is often more intuitive: how many months until attributable revenue covers fully-loaded cost?

Using the example above: $68,000 fully-loaded cost divided by $12,000 monthly attributable revenue (at full ramp) gives a payback period of roughly 5.7 months. That means the hire is cash-flow neutral partway through the year and cash-flow positive from that point forward.

During the ramp period (months one through three), monthly attributable revenue is lower, so the practical payback period is longer than the simple math suggests. Build that into your expectation.
