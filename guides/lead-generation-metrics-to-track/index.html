Lead Generation Metrics: What Should I Track?

Branch and Root Consulting · Orange County, CA · Updated June 2026

For a local service business, nine metrics cover everything worth measuring in a lead generation program: speed-to-lead, contact rate, qualification rate, appointments set, show rate, cost per qualified lead, lead-to-sale conversion, revenue per lead, and pipeline coverage. Track these and you have full visibility from first contact to closed job — and most owners have none of them, because nobody built the layer that captures each handoff automatically instead of requiring a manual weekly report.

The 9 metrics that actually drive decisions

1. Speed-to-lead. Time from a lead’s first contact to your first response. Industry data consistently shows that leads contacted within five minutes convert at dramatically higher rates than those reached an hour later. For inbound calls this is measured in seconds; for form submissions it should be minutes, not hours. Fix this first if contact rate is low.

2. Contact rate. The percentage of leads where you reach an actual person (not a voicemail). A healthy inbound contact rate for a responsive local service business typically lands in the 60–80% range; for cold outbound, industry surveys generally show 15–30% depending on channel and list quality. Low contact rate is almost always a speed-to-lead or coverage problem, not a message problem.

3. Qualification rate. Of the contacts you reach, what percentage meet your criteria for a real prospect? A high contact rate paired with a low qualification rate means you are reaching plenty of people who cannot or will not buy — usually a list problem.

4. Appointments set. The number of confirmed visits or calls booked per month. This is the core output metric for an outbound lead gen hire and the hand-off point to your sales or estimating team. Everything upstream exists to produce this number. For quota-setting purposes, this is the KPI to hold a hire accountable to — see the guide on monthly lead output benchmarks for typical ranges.

5. Show rate. The percentage of booked appointments where the prospect actually shows. A no-showed estimate wastes the tech’s time entirely. Show rates for in-home service businesses typically range from 70–90% when reminder sequences are in place and lower without them. Below 70%, add a confirmation call or text the morning before.

6. Cost per qualified lead (CPQL). Total spend on lead generation divided by qualified leads produced. This is the metric that makes channels comparable — an outbound hire at $5,500/month producing 40 qualified leads runs $137.50 CPQL; an ad campaign at $2,000 producing 60 genuine leads runs $33. Operators who track this don’t need to guess which channel to scale; owners who don’t are making that call on feel.

7. Lead-to-sale conversion rate. The percentage of leads that become closed jobs. The post on what percentage of leads should convert breaks down healthy ranges by source and intent. Track by lead source separately — blending inbound and outbound into one number obscures where the real leverage is.

8. Revenue per lead. Total attributable revenue divided by total leads in the same period — the number that ties lead gen to your P&L. An HVAC business with $8,000 attributable to 40 leads has $200 revenue per lead; at $50 CPQL, the program returns $4 for every $1 spent. Tracked monthly, this is the clearest signal of program health.

9. Pipeline coverage. Total value of active opportunities divided by monthly revenue target. A ratio of 3:1 or higher gives you enough buffer for normal fallout. Coverage below 2:1 is a warning sign that pipeline is running thin.

The vanity metrics worth ignoring

Impressions, raw LinkedIn connections, emails sent, and website traffic without conversion data all look like progress and change nothing. Activity volume is not output — a hire who sends 500 emails per month and books zero appointments is not performing well, and a traffic spike that produces no calls is not a lead gen win.

From the Field · Branch & Root

Our clients see all nine of these metrics on a single dashboard because the system records every call, response time, and booked job automatically. There is no weekly report to pull and no manual entry required — the owner checks a screen. That is what makes the numbers actionable instead of historical.

How to actually build this tracking without a data team

You do not need sophisticated software. A CRM with a lead source field, a call log, and a simple spreadsheet covers most of it. The discipline is in recording data consistently — every lead tagged with its source, every appointment logged, every closed job linked back to its origin. Our lead generation services include monthly reporting on these metrics so you always know where the pipeline stands. The real question is whether you can answer any of these nine numbers today without pulling records — or whether your current program is running blind.
