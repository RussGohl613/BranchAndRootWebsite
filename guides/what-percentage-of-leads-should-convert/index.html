What Percentage of Leads Should Actually Convert to Sales?

Branch and Root Consulting · Orange County, CA · Updated June 2026

There is no single answer, because conversion rate depends almost entirely on lead source and intent. An inbound emergency call from a homeowner whose AC failed in August converts to a booked job at a dramatically higher rate than a cold outbound list worked by an SDR — the same number applied to both would be meaningless. For inbound, intent-driven leads in the trades, industry surveys typically put lead-to-sale conversion in the 30–50% range; for cold outbound, that same number commonly falls to 5–15%. The right benchmark is a chain of rates — one for each handoff in your funnel — and most owners can’t see any of them because nobody built the layer that records what happens between the first call and the closed job.

Why lead source changes everything about the expected conversion rate

Conversion benchmarks that lump all leads together are not useful. The intent behind the lead is the dominant variable — think of it in two categories. High-intent inbound (a Google caller, a website form, a referral) means the prospect already wants a service and is evaluating you specifically; these convert to appointments at a high rate when handled promptly. Low-intent outbound (a cold call or email to a purchased list) requires multiple touches and a longer cycle; the lead-to-sale rate is structurally lower, but so is the cost-per-lead. If you are measuring a blended rate across both sources, the number is nearly impossible to diagnose — operators who close well track each source separately; everyone else has one blended number that hides whether the problem is the list, the channel, or the close process.

The math chain from lead to closed job

Conversion rate is the product of several rates stacked in sequence. For a local service business, the chain is: lead to contact (industry data puts responsive inbound businesses at 60–80% ); contact to appointment (70–90% for high-intent inbound, 10–25% for cold outbound ); appointment to quote (no-shows are real — a landscaping estimate that gets blown off wastes the tech’s time entirely); and quote to close (40–70% for local service businesses, depending on price point and follow-up discipline ). A business with 70% contact, 80% contact-to-appointment, 90% appointment-to-quote, and 50% quote-to-close has an overall lead-to-sale rate of roughly 25% — but if any one stage drops, the overall number collapses quickly.

When we build out a lead gen system, every handoff in this chain gets its own tracked field — speed-to-lead logged automatically, contact rate calculated from call records, appointment and show data pulled from the CRM. The owner sees each rate on a single dashboard, so when the overall number drops it takes about 30 seconds to find which stage broke. Without that layer, most owners can’t answer the question at all.

Where to look first when conversion is low

The math chain above tells you exactly where to look. Measure each rate separately and find the worst-performing stage. Chasing overall conversion rate without segmenting by stage wastes effort on the wrong fix.

The most common problem stage for local service businesses is lead-to-contact. Missed calls and slow form responses are common, and they torpedo everything downstream. If you have not already read the post on how many leads your business is losing, that is the right starting point for diagnosing this stage specifically.

The second most common problem is quote-to-close — usually a follow-up gap rather than a pricing problem. A quote without a follow-up sequence closes at a materially lower rate; the pros have that sequence built into the CRM so it runs whether the owner thinks about it or not. The third is contact-to-appointment: if many contacts decline to book, the issue is either lead qualification or how the appointment is positioned on the call. Listening to recorded calls is the fastest diagnosis.

What a realistic conversion improvement looks like in practice

Consider a plumbing company that takes 50 inbound calls per month. Their current contact rate is 60% (30 contacts), appointment rate is 70% (21 appointments), no-show rate is 20% (17 visited), and close rate is 50% (8 closed jobs). That is a 16% lead-to-sale rate on 50 leads.

If they fix the contact rate to 80% by implementing a missed-call text-back system, the chain becomes: 40 contacts, 28 appointments, 22 visited, 11 closed — a 22% lead-to-sale rate. Same 50 leads, no new marketing spend. That is three additional jobs per month from a single operational fix. Our service bundles include this kind of contact-rate work as a core component because it produces measurable lift before any new lead volume is added. The real question is whether you can see each rate in your chain right now — or whether you would have to reconstruct last month’s numbers from memory to know where yours are breaking down.
