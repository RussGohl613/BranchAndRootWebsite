How Many Leads Should a Lead Generation Person Produce per Month?

Branch and Root Consulting · Orange County, CA · Updated June 2026

For local service businesses in the trades, a fully ramped lead gen hire should produce 15–30 qualified appointments set per month — because raw contact volume means little if the appointments never show. Broader outbound SDR benchmarks run 40–80 new contacts worked per month for high-ticket, relationship-driven sales and up to 150–300 per month for transactional, shorter-cycle outreach. The right quota for your business depends on deal size, channel, list quality, and whether you are measuring contacts touched, leads generated, or appointments booked — and most owners cannot answer that last question because nobody ever built the layer that tracks it.

Why the range is so wide — the four variables that drive output

Every lead gen quota is shaped by the same short list of inputs. Understanding them lets you set a number your hire can actually hit.

  • Ticket size and sales cycle. A $300 HVAC tune-up can be booked in a single phone call. A $15,000 whole-home re-pipe involves a site visit, a quote, and follow-up. Higher-ticket services naturally yield fewer monthly bookings but each one is worth more.
  • Channel. Cold calling at full volume can touch 40–80 contacts per day. LinkedIn outreach, which involves list building and message sequencing, typically runs at a fraction of that cadence. Email sequences sit somewhere in between. The channel sets a ceiling before your hire ever starts.
  • List quality. A tight, verified list of homeowners in your service area who fit your ideal customer profile converts at a dramatically higher rate than a scraped, unverified list. The same effort produces more qualified leads when the list is clean.
  • Definition of "lead." This is the most common source of quota confusion. A name and number pulled from a directory is not a lead. A person who answered the phone and agreed to a call is a lead. A person who confirmed an appointment is a qualified appointment. Know which of these you are measuring.

What does a realistic monthly quota look like for a trades business?

For an HVAC, plumbing, landscaping, or electrical business operating in a defined local market, a fully ramped outbound specialist running phone and email outreach typically produces somewhere in the range of 20–40 qualified contacts per month, of which 15–30 convert to booked appointments. These numbers assume a clean list, a defined service area, and a hire who has completed at least 90 days of ramp.

During the first 60 days, expect those numbers to be 30–50% lower while your hire is still learning your offer, your geography, and your objection patterns. Holding a new hire to full-ramp quota on day 30 is how you lose good people to frustration — but giving a pass on activity metrics because you have no system to track them is how you fund a slow non-performer for three months before noticing.

On the high end, a well-resourced specialist with a warm list (past customers, referrals, quote-but-never-closed from your CRM) can sometimes double these ranges. That warm list is worth building deliberately — the post on how many leads your business is already losing is a useful starting point.

Qualified appointments: the KPI that actually matters

Raw lead count is the most commonly reported number and the least useful one. The metric that connects lead generation to your bottom line is qualified appointments set — a real person, confirmed for a specific date and time, who meets your minimum criteria for the job. Hold your hire accountable to this number; the lead generation metrics guide lays out target conversion rates at each step of the chain.

How to set a quota you can defend

Work backwards from your revenue goal rather than picking a number from a benchmark. The logic runs like this:

  1. What is your average job revenue?
  2. How many closed jobs do you need per month to hit your growth target?
  3. What is your current lead-to-close rate? (If you do not know, start tracking it now.)
  4. Divide the closed jobs needed by your close rate to find required appointments set.
  5. Add a buffer of 20–30% to account for no-shows and disqualified appointments.

That final number is your qualified-appointment quota — grounded in your actual business, not borrowed from a benchmark built on a different market. Our lead generation services are built around this same math.

What happens when the numbers fall short?

Before cutting a hire loose for missing quota, run through the variable list above. Low output is usually a list problem or a channel-fit problem before it is a people problem — the same hire on a warm residential list with a tested script will dramatically outperform the same person cold-calling a scraped directory. Without a tracking system, you cannot tell the difference between someone working hard against a broken process and one simply not doing the work.
