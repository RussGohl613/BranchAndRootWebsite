How to use this playbook

Every conversation moves through three stages. Know which stage you're in, and run the play for that stage.

The funnel

You are never "pitching." You are running a diagnostic that ends in a prescription. The three stages map to how much the prospect has engaged.

COLD

Earn the conversation

First touch — walk-in, call, video, text, email, or DM. Goal: a pattern interrupt that earns a few minutes or a booked call.

WARM

Diagnose & present

They're engaged. Run discovery, surface the leak, present the right offer, and handle objections.

CLOSING

Lock it in

Pick the onboarding date, anchor the price, set the follow-up. Leave with a committed next step.

Hooks — earn the first 30 seconds

A hook isn't about their problems yet — that comes later, in the pitch. The hook's only job is to stop the hang-up and earn you 30 to 45 seconds. Joke a little, ask about their day, or just be honest about what this is — anything that doesn't put them or us down. The second they say yes, pivot into a way in (below).

  • Keep it brief. Humor complements the value — it doesn't replace it.
  • Read the room. If they're serious, drop the joke and go professional fast.
  • Avoid risky topics. Never politics, religion, or anything personal.
  • Be authentic. Forced humor trips the fake-detector — only use a line that feels like Branch & Root.

The three ways in

Once a hook earns their attention, the way in is the angle you actually diagnose — a different door into the business conversation. Rotate through at least two per conversation; never all three, it reads like a checklist. Pick the one that matches what the prospect just told you.

WAY IN A

Database Health Check

"We run a complimentary analysis of your existing customer records to find dormant revenue sitting idle. Most businesses we work with are sitting on $15,000 to $40,000 in untouched reactivation value."

Use when: 2+ years of customers, slow season, an old list.
WAY IN B

Search & Voice Visibility Scan

"We do a live scan of how your business shows up across every major search and answer platform in your zip codes. If a new customer in Mission Viejo or RSM asks for what you do, we show you exactly what they see — and where you're invisible."

Use when: competition or being hard to find comes up.
WAY IN C

Local Multi-Generational Team

"Branch & Root is a two-generation local team. The dads handle the operational structure. The sons run the growth side. We're not a remote agency — we live here, we know these zip codes."

Use when: they're skeptical of remote or impersonal agencies.

Voice — how we sound in the field

  • Warm but bold. You're not asking for their business — you're showing them what they're leaving on the table.
  • Local and specific. Use their city, their zip codes, their competitors' category. Never generic.
  • Diagnose, don't pitch. Ask first. Let the gap become visible before you offer the bridge.
  • Plain language. Frame the work as "operational infrastructure," never tools or jargon. No hype words (unlock, supercharge, 10x, game-changer, limited spots). No exclamation points — let the calm carry the confidence.
Stage 1

Cold — earn the conversation

You have no relationship yet. The only goal is a pattern interrupt that buys you a diagnostic conversation or a booked call. Open with a local observation or a complimentary check — never "do you have a minute?"

GoalA booked 15-minute call, a complimentary scan/health-check request, or a committed next step. You are not closing here — you are earning the next conversation.

Walk-in · the opener

Walk in during a mid-morning lull. Make eye contact with whoever is up front. Smile. Lead with a complimentary check anchored in their business — not a sales line.

Pattern interrupt — say this

"Hey — I noticed you've been operating here a while. I do a quick complimentary database health check for established local businesses in the area. Takes about three minutes. You'd be surprised what's sitting dormant in your records." (Pause. Let them respond.)

Walk-in · the diagnostic sequence

Once they engage, move into light diagnosis. These are curiosity questions, not an interrogation — each one exposes a gap you're about to fill.

  • "How long have you been here in South OC?"Establishes tenure and how deep their database goes.
  • "When a new inquiry comes in after hours, what happens to it?"Exposes the response gap (Service 2).
  • "When's the last time you reached back out to customers you haven't seen in six to twelve months?"Exposes dormant revenue (Service 1).
  • "Do you have a good sense of how you show up when someone searches for what you do in Ladera Ranch or Mission Viejo right now?"Pivot to Way in B.
After two or three questions, transition. Don't rush — let the gap become visible, then offer the bridge. Hand off to Stage 2 · Warm to present.

Cold call · the opener

Read this first. Cold calling is fine for our own prospecting. It is not a service we sell — "no cold calling for clients" stays on the Hard-NO list. Keep it consultative: a complimentary check, not a pitch.

Lead with a quick, specific, complimentary offer. Say who you are and that you're local in the first breath. Get to the point — owners are busy.

Opener — say this

"Hi, is this [Owner]? — [Your Name] with Branch & Root here in South OC. I'll be quick. We run a complimentary database health check for established [trade] shops in [city], and most owners are surprised how much past-customer revenue is sitting dormant. No pitch — can I run yours and send you what we find?" (Pause. Let them talk.)

If they engage — one diagnostic

"Quick one — when a new inquiry comes in after hours, what happens to it right now?" (Listen, then offer the scan or a 15-minute call.)

Voicemail — they won't always pick up

"Hi [Owner], [Your Name] with Branch & Root here in South OC. We help local [trade] shops recover dormant past customers and respond to new leads in 90 seconds. I'll text you a quick free scan of your numbers — no pitch. Talk soon."

Cold video audit · pre-send

A cold video audit is a 90–120 second personalized screen-share sent by email, text, or DM. Before recording, research the business: pull their Google Business Profile, search their category in their city, test their name on Siri and Google voice search. Build the video around what you actually find.

Cold video audit · the 90-second script

Three beats: open with local specificity, show one real gap, close with a low-friction ask.

Open — first 15 seconds

"Hey [Name] — I'm [Your Name] with Branch & Root Consulting here in South OC. I'm doing complimentary visibility scans for established local service businesses in [their city]. I pulled yours up and there are two things I want to show you that are costing you money right now. This takes about 90 seconds."

Beat — show the gap (Way in B + Way in A)

"Here's what a customer sees when they search for [their service] in [their zip] — and here's where you're not showing up. And based on how long you've been operating, you likely have hundreds of past customers who've gone quiet. Most businesses in your category are sitting on $15K to $40K in dormant revenue they don't know about."

Close — the ask (no pitch)

"I'd love to do a quick 15-minute walk-through this week — no obligation, just the full picture. Reply to this and we'll get it on the calendar."

Follow-up text after sending

"Hey [Name] — just sent you a quick 90-second video on your search visibility. Short and to the point. Let me know if you'd like me to run the full scan — it's free and I'll have it back to you in 48 hours."

Cold text (SMS) · keep it short

One or two sentences. Say who you are, anchor local, make one low-friction ask. No link in the first text. Never more than two follow-ups.

First text

"Hi [Name] — [Your Name] with Branch & Root here in South OC. We run a free database health check for established [trade] shops — most are sitting on $15K–$40K in dormant past-customer revenue. Want me to run yours? No pitch."

Follow-up (48 hours, if no reply)

"No worries if now's not the time, [Name]. The scan is free and takes us 48 hours — just say the word and I'll send it over."

Cold email · subject + short body

The subject earns the open; the body earns the reply. Keep it scannable — a few short lines and one ask. No attachments on a cold send.

Subject line (pick one)

Body

"Hi [Name] — I'm [Your Name] with Branch & Root Consulting, a local team here in South OC. Follow-up (3–4 days, if no reply)

Cold DM (Instagram / Facebook) · casual, specific

Match the platform's tone — friendly and human, not corporate. Reference something real about their page or a recent post, then make one soft ask.

Opening DM

"Hey [Name] — saw [Business Name] come up for [trade] in [city]. We're a local South OC team that helps shops recover dormant past customers and respond to new leads in 90 seconds. Mind if I send a quick free scan of how you show up vs other [city] shops? No pitch."

Follow-up DM

"All good if you're slammed, [Name] — the scan's free and quick whenever you want it. Just say the word."

Stage 2

Warm — diagnose & present

They've agreed to talk. Run real discovery, then present the right offer in their own words. Listen for the signals before you prescribe.

GoalTwo or more confirmed gaps, then a clear recommendation the prospect agrees fits. You're building the case — the close comes next.

Discovery — the question bank

Open scheduled calls with curiosity, not an agenda: "Before I show you anything — what's the one operational or revenue challenge that keeps coming back, no matter what you try?" Then dig in.

  • "How are you handling new inquiries that come in after your team's hours?"Speed-to-lead gap → Instant Responder.
  • "When did you last run any outreach to customers you haven't seen in over a year?"Dormant database → Reactivation.
  • "If I searched for [their service] in [their main zip] right now, would you be confident in what I'd find?"Visibility gap → Local Growth Engine. Offer to run it live (Way in B).
  • "How much of your business is repeat customers versus brand-new?"Reveals whether the leak is retention or acquisition.
  • "Where do you want to be in 12–18 months — more capacity filled, more locations, a specific area you want to own?"Ambition signal → escalation to Full Engine.
Listen for: after-hours lead loss, a dormant database, weak or inconsistent online presence, heavy reliance on referrals with no follow-up. When you hear two or more, you have your opening.

Present the offer — bundle or single service

Decide on the spot which way to go, then use the matching script. The two bundles and the three individual services are below side by side.

Decision guide. Start at The Foundation for most established businesses. Escalate to The Full Engine when you hear two or more ambition signals. Sell a single service when they have just one clear gap or a smaller budget and want a low-commitment entry.

The Foundation — Service 1 (Customer Reactivation Engine) + Service 2 (Instant Responder System) + a lead-capture page. $2,500 setup + $697/mo flat. No contracts, no ad spend. Most popular starting point.

Present it as one system

"Based on what you're describing, you're in a really common spot for established businesses here — you've built something real, but revenue is quietly leaking in two places. The first is your existing customer list. The second is your response window to new inquiries. We put together a bundled system that closes both at once, without adding work for you or your team. Service 1 re-engages your dormant database in the first 30 days — no ad spend, customers who already trust you. Service 2 responds to every new lead within 90 seconds, day or night. Together they recover what you've already earned and stop you from losing what's new. That's the Foundation — $2,500 to build, $697 a month flat after that."

ROI anchor: most Foundation clients recover the full setup cost within the first reactivation campaign — often inside 30 to 45 days.

The Full Engine — Foundation + Service 3 (Local Growth Engine: custom website + SEO/AEO + Facebook & Instagram ads). $3,500 automation setup + a website project fee quoted per engagement, then $1,497–$1,997/mo. Ad budget is paid directly to Meta by the client.

Present it as market ownership

"What you're describing is exactly the profile we built the Full Engine for. The Foundation plugs the leaks — recovers your database and captures every inbound lead. The Full Engine builds the moat on top of it: a conversion-built website, visibility across Google and AI answer tools, and managed Facebook and Instagram ads that bring in a predictable flow of new local customers. The dads keep the operational systems sound, the sons run the growth at full speed, and you get both for one flat monthly number. Setup is $3,500 plus the website build, then $1,497 to $1,997 a month depending on what's active."

Qualify before you promise ads. The Meta ads piece needs a minimum of $1,500/mo in client ad spend to work — below that, the algorithm can't optimize. This is a service-agreement rule. If they can't commit that spend, sell the website + SEO/AEO and the Foundation, and add ads later.

Objection handling

You will hear these five every time. Tap to open the response. Acknowledge, reframe, return to the diagnostic — never argue.

1"Let me talk to my spouse first."
Response

"Absolutely — that's exactly the right call for a real business decision. Here's what I'd ask you to share with them: the cost of not fixing this. Every month you wait, customers in your database are calling someone else because they didn't hear from you, and after-hours inquiries are gone by morning. Let me put together a one-page summary of what we found and what it would cost — so when you sit down together, you're looking at real numbers, not a concept. Would a quick 15-minute call with both of you later this week help?"

2"I don't have time for this right now."
Response

"That's exactly why we built it this way. Your total time is one short onboarding call — we handle everything else. We're not asking you to run a campaign, we're asking you to let us run it for you. The time cost of doing nothing is the part that keeps compounding. If now isn't right, when is — I want to catch you before your peak season hits."

3"We already have a local web guy."
Response

"That's great, and we're not here to replace a relationship you trust. Most web folks build the front end — the site itself. What we build is the communication infrastructure behind it: database reactivation, 90-second lead response, automated follow-up. Different lane entirely. Can I ask — does your web guy also handle the reactivation and the inbound response side? If he does, great. If not, that's the piece we fill."

4"We tried digital ads before and got burned."
Response

"Fair concern — and most businesses got burned because someone sold them ads without building the infrastructure to convert the traffic. The Foundation has zero ads. We start by fixing the foundation — recovering existing customers and making sure new leads get captured and answered. If you ever want paid traffic later, the system is already built to handle it. That experience is actually a reason to start here, not a reason to avoid us."

5"We get all our business from word-of-mouth."
Response

"Word-of-mouth is the best foundation you can have — it means your customers love you. Here's the risk most word-of-mouth businesses don't see: referrals still Google you before they call. If your online presence is thin or your response time is slow, you're losing a share of those warm referrals before they ever reach you. We're not replacing your word-of-mouth — we're making sure it converts."

Stage 3

Closing — lock it in

The value is established. Now make the next step frictionless, anchor the price, and never leave without a committed action.

GoalAn onboarding call booked on the calendar, the intake form sent, and a follow-up scheduled — before you leave the room or end the call.

The frictionless onboarding close

Close — say this

"Here's how easy this is to start. We do one short onboarding call — you, me, and our ops lead. We collect your existing customer data and your basic business info, and from there we build everything. You don't touch it. Your first reactivation campaign goes out within two weeks. Setup is $2,500 one time, and $697 a month after that — flat, no surprises. I've got Tuesday at 10 or Thursday at 2 for the onboarding call. Which works better?"

  • Offer two specific scheduling windows on the spot. Don't ask "when works" — give a choice between two times.
  • Have the onboarding intake form ready on your phone, or a printed one-pager to hand over.
  • Do not leave without a committed next step: a call booked, a follow-up scheduled, or a one-pager left with your direct number circled.

Pricing-confidence anchors

When price comes up — and it will — reframe it as a deposit on revenue, not a cost.

  • Foundation setup ($2,500): "Most clients recover this in the first reactivation campaign. It's not a cost — it's a deposit on revenue you've already earned."
  • Foundation monthly ($697): "Less than one lost lead per week. That's what this prevents."
  • Full Engine setup ($3,500 + website): "This is the infrastructure build for a growth company. You're not buying a service — you're building an asset."
  • Full Engine monthly ($1,497–$1,997): "For a business your size, that's the overhead of a half-time marketing employee — with none of the management burden."
  • If they hesitate on price: "Let me ask you this — if we recover $20,000 in dormant customer revenue in the first 30 days, has this paid for itself?" (Pause.)

Tier escalation — when to move up to the Full Engine

Start every prospect at the Foundation. Escalate only when you hear two or more of these ambition signals — otherwise the Full Engine feels oversized and they disengage.

"I want to grow into [another area]" "I have more capacity than I'm filling" "I've wanted to do more marketing but don't know where to start" "My competitor seems to be everywhere lately" "I want to step back from day-to-day eventually" "We've been stable but I want to actually scale"

Guardrails — the hard "no" list

When a prospect asks for something on this list, don't improvise a workaround. Acknowledge, redirect, and return to what the system does deliver. Selling outside these lines creates delivery failures.

  • No manual social community management. "Our content system is automated and engineered for lead generation — we don't manually manage feeds or reply to comments. That's a different category."
  • No traditional cold calling — as a service. We don't run manual outbound telemarketing for clients; say "our outreach model is digital-first and data-driven." (Our own sales team may cold-call prospects — see the Cold stage. That's separate from what we sell.)
  • No isolated web builds. "Every site we build is wired into the lead-capture and follow-up pipeline by design. A standalone site without that infrastructure isn't something we offer."
  • No fabricated proof. We never publish invented review counts, ratings, or credentials. Verify real numbers before anything goes live.

Follow-up cadence

  • Within 2 hours: send the calendar invite and a warm recap email confirming the onboarding date and what to bring (customer-list export or CRM access).
  • At 48 hours: one sentence — "Any questions on the form? I want to make sure we hit your busy season ready to go."
  • Day 5 (if no response): "[Name] — I don't want this to fall through the cracks. Can we confirm the onboarding call this week?"
