What Percentage of Leads Should Actually Convert to Sales?

Branch and Root Consulting · Orange County, CA · Updated June 2026

There is no single answer, because conversion rate depends almost entirely on lead source and intent. An inbound emergency call from a homeowner whose AC failed in August converts to a booked job at a dramatically higher rate than a cold outbound list worked by an SDR — the same number applied to both would be meaningless. For inbound, intent-driven leads in the trades, industry surveys typically put lead-to-sale conversion in the 30–50% range; for cold outbound, that same number commonly falls to 5–15%. The right benchmark is not a single percentage — it is a chain of rates, one for each handoff in your funnel.

Why lead source changes everything about the expected conversion rate

Conversion benchmarks that lump all leads together are not useful. The intent behind the lead is the dominant variable. Think of it in two categories:

High-intent inbound: Someone called your number from a Google search, submitted a contact form from your website, or was referred directly by a past customer. They already want a service; they are evaluating whether to use you specifically. These leads convert to appointments at a high rate when handled promptly, and appointments close at a high rate because the buyer intent is genuine.

Low-intent outbound: Someone on a purchased or scraped list who received a cold call or email. They did not raise their hand. Converting these contacts requires multiple touches, a clear value proposition, and a longer sales cycle. The lead-to-sale rate is structurally lower, but so is the cost-per-lead, which is why the economics can still work if the math is tracked correctly.

If you are measuring a blended conversion rate across both sources, the number will be pulled toward whichever source makes up more of your volume — and diagnosing problems with that blended number is nearly impossible. Segment by source first.

The math chain from lead to closed job

Conversion rate is not a single number — it is the product of several rates stacked in sequence. For a local service business, the chain typically looks like this:

  1. Lead to contact: What percentage of leads do you actually reach? Missed calls, unread forms, and unreturned voicemails kill conversion here before anyone ever gets a chance to sell. Industry data typically puts contact rates for responsive businesses in the 60–80% range for inbound.
  2. Contact to appointment: Of the contacts you reach, what percentage agree to a visit or quote? For high-intent inbound this is often 70–90%; for cold outbound it may be 10–25%.
  3. Appointment to quote: Of booked appointments, how many show up and result in a quote being submitted? No-show rates matter here. A landscaping estimate that gets no-showed wastes the tech's time and resets the clock on that lead.
  4. Quote to close: Of submitted quotes, what percentage are accepted? This is where price, trust, and follow-up intersect. For local service businesses, close rates on submitted quotes commonly range from 40–70% depending on price point and competition.

Multiply those four rates together to get your true lead-to-sale conversion. A business with 70% contact, 80% contact-to-appointment, 90% appointment-to-quote, and 50% quote-to-close has an overall lead-to-sale rate of roughly 25%. That is a healthy number for inbound leads — but if any one of those four rates drops, the overall number collapses quickly.

Where to look first when conversion is low

The math chain above tells you exactly where to look. Measure each rate separately and find the worst-performing stage. Chasing overall conversion rate without segmenting by stage wastes effort on the wrong fix.

The most common problem stage for local service businesses is lead-to-contact. Missed calls and slow form responses are common, and they torpedo everything downstream. If you have not already read the post on how many leads your business is losing, that is the right starting point for diagnosing this stage specifically.

The second most common problem is quote-to-close — usually a follow-up gap rather than a pricing problem. A quote without a follow-up sequence closes at a materially lower rate. The third is contact-to-appointment: if many contacts decline to book, the issue is either lead qualification or how the appointment is positioned on the call. Listening to recorded calls is the fastest diagnosis.

What a realistic conversion improvement looks like in practice

Consider a plumbing company that takes 50 inbound calls per month. Their current contact rate is 60% (30 contacts), appointment rate is 70% (21 appointments), no-show rate is 20% (17 visited), and close rate is 50% (8 closed jobs). That is a 16% lead-to-sale rate on 50 leads.

If they fix the contact rate to 80% by implementing a missed-call text-back system, the chain becomes: 40 contacts, 28 appointments, 22 visited, 11 closed — a 22% lead-to-sale rate. Same 50 leads, no new marketing spend. That is three additional jobs per month from a single operational fix.
