Lead Generation Metrics: What Should I Track?

Branch and Root Consulting · Orange County, CA · Updated June 2026

For a local service business, nine metrics cover everything worth measuring in a lead generation program: speed-to-lead, contact rate, qualification rate, appointments set, show rate, cost per qualified lead, lead-to-sale conversion, revenue per lead, and pipeline coverage. Track these and you have full visibility from first contact to closed job. Track anything less and you will have blind spots; track more and you will be drowning in numbers that do not change decisions.

The 9 metrics that actually drive decisions

1. Speed-to-lead. The time between a lead's first contact and your first response. Industry data consistently shows that response time is one of the strongest predictors of contact rate — leads contacted within five minutes convert at dramatically higher rates than those contacted an hour later. For inbound calls this is measured in seconds; for form submissions it should be measured in minutes, not hours. This is the first metric to fix if contact rate is low.

2. Contact rate. The percentage of leads where you reach an actual person (not a voicemail). A healthy inbound contact rate for a responsive local service business typically lands in the 60–80% range; for cold outbound, industry surveys generally show 15–30% depending on channel and list quality. Low contact rate is almost always a speed-to-lead or coverage problem, not a message problem.

3. Qualification rate. Of the contacts you reach, what percentage meet your criteria for a real prospect? A high contact rate paired with a low qualification rate means you are reaching plenty of people who cannot or will not buy — usually a list problem.

4. Appointments set. The number of confirmed visits or calls booked per month. This is the core output metric for an outbound lead gen hire and the hand-off point to your sales or estimating team. Everything upstream exists to produce this number. For quota-setting purposes, this is the KPI to hold a hire accountable to — see the guide on monthly lead output benchmarks for typical ranges.

5. Show rate. The percentage of booked appointments where the prospect actually shows up. A landscaping estimate appointment that gets no-showed wastes the estimator's time entirely. Show rates for in-home service businesses typically range from 70–90% when reminder sequences are in place and lower without them. If show rate is below 70%, add a confirmation call or text the day before.

6. Cost per qualified lead (CPQL). Total spend on lead generation (salary, tools, ad spend, agency fees) divided by the number of qualified leads produced in the same period. This is the metric that makes different channels comparable. An outbound hire who costs $5,500 per month and produces 40 qualified leads has a CPQL of $137.50. An ad campaign that costs $2,000 and produces 60 leads has a CPQL of $33 — but only if those 60 leads are genuinely qualified.

7. Lead-to-sale conversion rate. The percentage of leads that become closed jobs. The post on what percentage of leads should convert breaks down healthy ranges by source and intent. Track by lead source separately — blending inbound and outbound into one number obscures where the real leverage is.

8. Revenue per lead. Total attributable revenue divided by total leads in the same period. This is the single number that ties your lead gen program to your P&L. An HVAC business with $8,000 in monthly revenue attributable to 40 leads has an $200 revenue per lead. If CPQL is $50, the program is generating $4 in revenue for every $1 spent. This ratio, tracked monthly, is the clearest signal of whether the program is healthy.

9. Pipeline coverage. Total value of active opportunities divided by your monthly revenue target. A coverage ratio of 3:1 or higher means you have three times your target in active quotes — enough buffer for normal fallout. Coverage below 2:1 is a warning sign that pipeline is running thin and lead volume needs to increase.

The vanity metrics worth ignoring

Some numbers look impressive in a report and change nothing. For a local service business, the main ones to deprioritize are:

  • Impressions and reach. How many people saw your ad or post. Completely disconnected from whether anyone called or booked.
  • Raw connection counts on LinkedIn. Connections that never become conversations produce no revenue. Acceptance rate only matters as a leading indicator of the conversation rate downstream.
  • Emails sent. Activity volume is not output. A hire who sends 500 emails per month and books zero appointments is not performing well.
  • Website traffic without conversion data. Traffic alone does not pay the bills. Sessions matter only when you know what percentage converted to a contact or call.

How to actually build this tracking without a data team

You do not need sophisticated software. A CRM with a lead source field, a call log, and a simple spreadsheet covers most of it. The discipline is in recording data consistently — every lead tagged with its source, every appointment logged, every closed job linked back to its origin.
