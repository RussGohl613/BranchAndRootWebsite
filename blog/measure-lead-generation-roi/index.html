How to Measure Lead Generation ROI From a New Hire

Branch and Root Consulting · Orange County, CA · Updated June 2026

Lead generation ROI is calculated as (revenue attributable to the hire − fully-loaded cost of the hire) ÷ fully-loaded cost of the hire, expressed as a percentage. A hire who generates $120,000 in attributable revenue against $60,000 in fully-loaded annual cost produces a 100% ROI — meaning you doubled the investment. The formula is simple; the hard part is defining "fully-loaded cost" and "attributable revenue" honestly, because undercounting either one produces numbers that look good on paper but mislead real decisions.

What goes into fully-loaded cost

Most owners undercount the cost side. Salary is the obvious line item, but a fully-loaded cost calculation includes everything the hire actually costs the business to employ and equip:

  • Base salary or wages. The number on the offer letter.
  • Payroll taxes and employer benefits. In most US markets, employer-side payroll taxes, health insurance contributions, and any retirement match add 20–30% to the base salary cost. A $50,000 salary commonly costs $60,000–$65,000 when fully burdened.
  • Tool licenses. CRM seats, phone dialers, list-building tools, email platforms. These are real recurring costs that belong in the calculation.
  • Recruiting and onboarding. Job board fees, your own time spent interviewing, and any onboarding training all have a dollar cost. Spread these over a 12-month period for the annual ROI calculation.
  • Management overhead. Your time spent reviewing calls, running pipeline meetings, and coaching the hire has an opportunity cost. Estimate it honestly.

For a typical full-time outbound hire at a local service business, fully-loaded annual cost commonly lands in the $65,000–$85,000 range once all of the above are included. Use that number as your denominator, not the base salary alone.

How to define attributable revenue

Revenue attribution is the other side of the equation, and it requires agreeing on a clear rule before the hire starts — not after a dispute about credit.

The most defensible method for a local service business is lead-source tagging: every job in your CRM or job management system carries a lead source field. If the appointment came from your lead gen hire's outreach, the resulting job revenue is tagged to that source. At month end, you sum the closed revenue from that tag.

A few decisions you need to make in advance:

  • Attribution window. How long after first contact does a closed job still count as attributable? For a same-day HVAC repair, this may be one week. For a full landscaping installation that takes four months from first call to signed contract, you need a longer window. Define it before any disputes arise.
  • Partial vs. full credit. If the hire set the appointment but you personally closed the job, does the hire get full credit? Most owners attribute 100% to the hire since the appointment creation is the function being measured.
  • Repeat business. If a customer your hire originated books again six months later through word of mouth, that repeat revenue is typically not attributed to the hire. Keep first-job attribution clean.

A worked example with round numbers

The numbers below are illustrative only — they are constructed to show the math clearly, not to represent actual benchmarks for your industry or market.

Suppose you hire an outbound specialist at $50,000 base salary. With employer-side costs and tool licenses, the fully-loaded annual cost is $68,000. During the first year, the hire sets 180 qualified appointments. Your close rate on those appointments is 40%, producing 72 closed jobs. Your average job revenue is $2,000, so attributable annual revenue is $144,000.

Plugging into the formula: ($144,000 − $68,000) ÷ $68,000 = 112% ROI. The hire more than doubled its cost in year one.

Now change one variable: drop the close rate from 40% to 20% because follow-up is inconsistent after the appointment is set. Attributable revenue falls to $72,000. ROI becomes ($72,000 − $68,000) ÷ $68,000 = 6%. Barely breakeven — and that ignores the ramp period where output was even lower.

The lesson is that lead gen ROI is highly sensitive to what happens after the appointment is booked. A hire who sets great appointments against a weak close process will always look expensive. That is a sales problem, not a lead gen problem — and the post on diagnosing whether you have a lead gen or sales problem is worth reading before drawing conclusions from the numbers.

Payback period: a simpler lens for the first year

ROI percentages are useful for comparison, but payback period is often more intuitive: how many months until attributable revenue covers fully-loaded cost?

Using the example above: $68,000 fully-loaded cost divided by $12,000 monthly attributable revenue (at full ramp) gives a payback period of roughly 5.7 months. That means the hire is cash-flow neutral partway through the year and cash-flow positive from that point forward.

During the ramp period (months one through three), monthly attributable revenue is lower, so the practical payback period is longer than the simple math suggests. Build that into your expectation.
